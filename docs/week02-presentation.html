Stage 1: Empathize

Why Empathy Matters

  • Avoid assumptions
  • Discover hidden needs
  • Build better solutions

Empathy Techniques

  • User Interviews
  • Observation
  • Empathy Mapping

Empathy Map

  • Says: Direct quotes
  • Thinks: Unstated thoughts
  • Does: Observed behaviors
  • Feels: Emotions during interaction

Goal: Deep understanding of user context

Stage 2: Define

ÂÆöÁæ© - ÂïèÈ°å„ÅÆÊòéÁ¢∫Âåñ

Point of View (POV) Statement

[User type] needs [need/goal]
because [insight from research]
but currently [current problem/limitation]

Characteristics of Good Problem Statements

  • User-Focused: About people, not technology
  • Specific: Clear and actionable
  • Insight-Based: Based on real research
  • Problem-Oriented: Describes problem, not solution

Stage 3: Ideate

Áô∫ÊÉ≥ - „ÇΩ„É™„É•„Éº„Ç∑„Éß„É≥„ÅÆÁîüÊàê

Ideation Principles

  • Quantity Over Quality: Generate many ideas first
  • Defer Judgment: No criticism during brainstorming
  • Build on Ideas: "Yes, and..." instead of "Yes, but..."
  • Encourage Wild Ideas: Crazy ideas can lead to breakthroughs

Creative Techniques

Rapid Sketching

  • 8-10 quick ideas, 1 minute each
  • Focus on concepts, not perfection

"What If" Scenarios

  • Remove constraints
  • Change context
  • Reverse assumptions

User-Centered Design Principles

„É¶„Éº„Ç∂„Éº‰∏≠ÂøÉ„Éá„Ç∂„Ç§„É≥ÂéüÂâá

1. Know Your Users

  • User research
  • Personas
  • Context of use

2. Design for Usability

  • Efficiency
  • Effectiveness
  • Satisfaction

3. Iterate on Feedback

  • Test early and often
  • Learn from failures
  • Continuous improvement

Design Principles in Practice

  • Visibility: Clear affordances and navigation
  • Feedback: Immediate response and clear status
  • Consistency: Similar actions work similarly

Design Thinking vs. Traditional Design

Traditional Approach

  • Solution-First
  • Assumption-Based
  • Linear Process
  • Expert-Driven

Design Thinking

  • Problem-First
  • Research-Based
  • Iterative Process
  • User-Driven

Benefits of Design Thinking

  • Better Solutions: Solve real problems
  • Reduced Risk: Test before investing heavily
  • User Satisfaction: People want to use the product
  • Innovation: Breakthrough solutions emerge

Common Design Thinking Mistakes

1. Skipping Empathy

Problem: Jump straight to solutions

Solution: Always start with user research

2. Solution-Focused Problem Statements

Problem: "Students need a better search function"

Better: "Students need to quickly find courses that match requirements"

3. Judging Ideas Too Early

Problem: "That won't work" during brainstorming

Solution: Generate many ideas first, evaluate later

Common Questions

  • "How do I know if my problem statement is good?"
    It should be user-focused, specific, and based on real insights
  • "What if I can't think of enough ideas?"
    Use creative techniques like "worst possible idea" or analogies
  • "How detailed should my prototype be?"
    Low-fidelity is perfect - focus on concepts, not visual details
