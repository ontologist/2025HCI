Why User Research Matters

What Happens Without Research

  • Assumptions replace facts
  • Solutions don't match problems
  • Resources wasted
  • User frustration

What Research Provides

  • Evidence-based decisions
  • Deep user understanding
  • Problem validation
  • Hidden insights

Types of User Research

Qualitative Research

Understanding why and how

  • Methods: Interviews, observations
  • Data: Words, behaviors, emotions
  • Question: "Why do students find registration frustrating?"
  • When: Early research, understanding context

Quantitative Research

Measuring what and how many

  • Methods: Surveys, analytics, A/B testing
  • Data: Numbers, statistics
  • Question: "How many students abandon registration?"
  • When: Validating designs, measuring impact

Key Insight: What people say ‚â† What people do

User Interview Fundamentals

Why Interviews Are Powerful

  • Rich, detailed insights
  • Flexibility to follow interesting leads
  • Emotional data
  • Build empathy
  • Discover the unknown

Semi-Structured Interviews (Recommended)

  • Guide with flexibility
  • Core questions + follow-ups
  • Balance consistency and exploration

Interview Structure

1. Introduction (2-3 min)

Build rapport, explain purpose, set expectations, get permission

2. Warm-Up Questions (3-5 min)

Easy, non-threatening questions to build comfort

3. Main Questions (15-20 min)

Core research topics, open-ended questions, deep exploration

4. Wrap-Up (2-3 min)

"Anything else?" Thank participant, explain next steps

The Art of Asking Questions

Avoid: Closed Questions

  • ‚ùå "Do you like the system?"
  • ‚ùå "Is it fast or slow?"
  • ‚ùå "Would you use a mobile app?"

Use: Open-Ended Questions

  • ‚úÖ "Tell me about your last registration experience"
  • ‚úÖ "Walk me through how you choose courses"
  • ‚úÖ "What's the most frustrating part?"

Critical Probing Questions

  • "Why?"
  • "Can you tell me more about that?"
  • "Can you give me an example?"
  • "What happened next?"

Active Listening Skills

Most Important Interview Skill: Actually Listening

1. Give Full Attention
  • Put away distractions
  • Make eye contact
  • Don't interrupt
2. Let Silence Work
  • Don't fear silence
  • Count to 5 before filling gaps
  • Leads to deeper insights
3. Watch Body Language
  • Frustration: Sighs, tension
  • Confusion: Pauses, hesitation
  • Excitement: Leaning forward, smiling
4. Take Good Notes
  • Record direct quotes
  • Note behaviors
  • Capture emotions

Observation Methods

Learning by Watching

"What people do" often differs from "what people say"

Direct Observation

  • Watch users in natural environment
  • Don't interfere or help
  • Note what they do, not what they say

Contextual Inquiry

  • Combination of observation + interview
  • Watch them work, ask questions during process
  • Best for understanding workflows

Think-Aloud Protocol

  • Users verbalize thoughts while performing tasks
  • Reveals mental models and confusion points
  • Great for usability testing

Surveys: When and How

Surveys are Good For:

  • Large sample sizes (100+ participants)
  • Quantifying patterns
  • Validating assumptions
  • Reaching distributed users

Surveys are BAD For:

  • Understanding "why"
  • Initial exploration
  • Discovering unknown problems
  • Deep insights

Survey Design Best Practices

  • Keep it short: 5-10 minutes max
  • Clear, simple language
  • Avoid leading questions
  • Test before distributing

Analyzing Qualitative Data

Affinity Mapping Process

1. Extract Insights

Write each finding on separate note

2. Group Similar Items

Look for patterns, cluster related insights

3. Label Themes

Name each cluster descriptively

4. Prioritize

How many users? How severe?

Finding Patterns

  • Frequency: Mentioned by 4/5 users ‚Üí Important
  • Emotional Intensity: Strong frustration ‚Üí High priority
  • Surprises: Unexpected findings ‚Üí Valuable insights

Synthesizing User Needs

From Findings to Actionable Needs

User Needs Statement Framework

[User type] needs to [goal/action]
in order to [desired outcome]
because [current challenge/context]

Characteristics of Good User Needs

  • User-Focused: About users, not systems
  • Specific: Clear and concrete
  • Evidence-Based: Supported by research data
  • Problem-Oriented: Not solution-oriented

Informed Consent

  • Explain purpose, duration, data usage
  • Get permission (verbal or written)
  • Right to withdraw anytime

Privacy and Confidentiality

  • Anonymize data
  • Secure storage
  • Use pseudonyms in reports

Avoiding Bias

  • Don't lead participants
  • Stay neutral
  • Include diverse participants

Common Research Mistakes

1. Leading Questions

‚úÖ "How do you feel about the interface?"

2. Asking About Future Behavior

‚ùå "Would you use a mobile version?"

3. Talking Too Much

80/20 rule: Participant talks 80%, you talk 20%
